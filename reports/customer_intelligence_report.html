01 / EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Executive Summary

This report presents a structured customer intelligence analysis for a direct-to-consumer (D2C) fashion brand operating across the United States with approximately 3,900 customers. The founding team asked a single, high-stakes question: "Is the business building a loyal customer base, or is it reliant on continuous promotional activity — and what should we do about it?"

Using only transactional and behavioural data (no loyalty scores, no churn labels, no timestamps), we engineered custom metrics to define value, measure satisfaction, and quantify promo dependency. We then built a segmentation layer in SQL to extract actionable intelligence across customers, geographies, and product categories.

Core Findings

⚠ Promo Dependency Is High but Misdirected 43% of all purchases used a discount or promo code. Critically, loyal customers (≥38 prior purchases) with low promo reliance spend more on average ($60.58) than heavily promo-reliant buyers ($60.04). The discount programme is subsidising purchases that would have happened anyway.
✓ Organic Geographic Pull Exists Arizona, Virginia, Michigan, Tennessee, and Texas all show above-average spend with promo dependency below 40% — clear indicators of genuine brand affinity that has never been deliberately targeted.
✗ Three Segments Are Margin Drains Summer Clothing, Summer Footwear, and Fall Outerwear combine high promo reliance (45%+) with a 30–32% concentration of Low-value customers. These are prime candidates for a structured promotional sunset.

Strategic Recommendation

Phase out category-wide public discounts in the three identified sunset segments over six weeks, replacing them with behavioural triggers (e.g., 48-hour cart abandonment → free shipping). Simultaneously, shift acquisition spend toward Male Gen X / Boomer customers in organic-pull geographies, using Accessories as the entry-point hook — the category with the highest average customer tenure.

02 / METHODOLOGY

Analytical Methodology

Dataset & Constraints

The raw dataset contains 3,900 rows with 18 columns per customer: demographics (age, gender, location), transaction details (item, category, spend, season), behavioural signals (frequency, previous purchases, subscription status), and promotional usage (discount applied, promo code used, shipping type).

Key constraint: No loyalty score, no churn label, and no timestamps exist. Every analytical concept must be constructed from available variables.

Feature Engineering

FeatureFormula / LogicBusiness Purpose
dependency_score (Discount_Flag + Promo_Flag) ÷ 2 Measures promo reliance per transaction (0 = organic, 1 = fully incentivised)
satisfaction_flag Review Rating ≥ 4.0 → 1 else 0 Binary high-satisfaction signal (consistent with NPS 'promoter' zone)
freq_numeric Frequency label → annual purchase count Normalises frequency strings to a comparable numeric scale
composite_value_score 40% tenure + 40% freq_rank + 20% spend_rank Holistic value proxy weighting proven behaviour over single-transaction spend
value_tier Top 25% → High; 40–75% → Medium; Bottom → Low Actionable customer tier for targeted strategy
loyalty_volume Previous Purchases ≥ 75th pct (≈38) Definition A: proven historical repeat behaviour
loyalty_frequency freq_numeric ≥ 26 AND satisfaction_flag = 1 Definition B: engaged, satisfied, high-frequency buyer

Loyalty Definition Validation

Why Volume-Based Loyalty Was Chosen Pearson correlation with Purchase Amount: Volume-Based = +0.0142 vs Frequency-Based = −0.0116. The Volume model is directionally positive and internally consistent — customers with more past purchases have proven they return independently of promotional incentives. The Frequency model inadvertently captures recent-only behaviour which is more susceptible to seasonal noise.

04 / RETENTION PLAYBOOK

Retention Playbook

1. Promotional Sunset Plan

The data identifies three specific category × season combinations as prime sunset candidates: Summer Clothing, Summer Footwear, and Fall Outerwear. These segments show the highest convergence of high promo reliance (45%+) and a disproportionate share of Low-value customers (30–32%).

Trade-off Removing public discounts may cause a 10–15% short-term volume drop in these segments as bargain hunters churn. However, since these customers are disproportionately low-value with shallow tenure, the impact on total LTV is minimal. Net margin per transaction will rise by the discount rate (estimated 15–20%).
PhaseTimelineActionMetric to Track
1. A/B TestWeek 1–250% of traffic sees no category discount in sunset segmentsNet margin $ per cohort
2. EvaluateWeek 3–4If B-test margin > A-test, expand to 80% of traffic90-day repeat purchase rate
3. Full SunsetWeek 5–6Remove all public % discounts. Replace with 48h cart-abandon → free shipping triggerAvg order value, churn rate by segment

Trigger Behaviour: Instead of public seasonal sales, implement personalised behavioural triggers. A customer who adds Summer Footwear to cart and leaves for 48 hours receives a free shipping offer — which costs the brand ~$4–5 vs a 15–20% discount that costs $9–12 on a $60 AOV.

Summary Recommendation The business is partially reliant on promotional activity, but this dependency is concentrated in specific, low-value segments — not spread across the entire customer base. The strategic action is not to eliminate promos entirely, but to surgically remove them from segments where they are not generating long-term value, and redirect that budget into acquiring more customers who look like the high-value ICP defined above.
